Week 5 - Pairing and Feedback

Pairing is an integral part of DBC. That's not to say that it doesn't have challenges and pitfalls, but to me it seems like it's part of the learning process here at DBC. From my own experiences there have been times when the pairing session have been very helpful both individually and as a member of the group. Overall, I have found my pairs to be very easy to work with and sharing of what they know.

It has been rewarding to come to a challenge we both we unsure of how to handle and see how we eventually move through it. Sometimes we may have needed a break for a 1/2 hour other times it was just trying different things, but we eventually were able to push through. I have had a sesion or two where we were both able to come up with our own solution, but we then took time to refactor each others code and share our insights into how we were working together.

I have also had challenging pairing sessions. Ones where I or my parter wasn't grasping the material and it was really hard to move forward with the challenge. Somtimes we were able to flounder through the challenge or one or the other of us took the helm to head the challenge. I have to say, from my perspective, it is really frustrating when it has been me that isn't up to par on the material.

The feedback has been helpful for the most part. Although, I dread getting feedback from someone when I know I wasn't up to par with the challenge even that feedback has been positive and even motivational. I enjoy giving feedback as well. Initially, it was a challenge to come up with a constructive comment, but through experience that has become easier for me as well.

Week 4 - Tech Problems - Net Neutrality

This is a large topic and one that I will only likely be able to decant here a little. From publicknowledge.org - Net Neutrality is defined as the principle that Internet service providers and governments should treat all data on the Internet equally, not discriminating or charging differentially by user, content, site, platform, application, type of attached equipment, and modes of communication.

Net Neutrality is an essential part of what is called the Open Internet. The Open Internet is a place where policy and open web standards allow the free flow of information and business without interference by a third party.From the Net Neutrality article on wikipedia.org: An Open Internet means full resources of the Internet and means to operate on it are easily accessible to all individuals and companies

A Closed Internet is just the opposite, in that it favors certian types of data or usage.

The resulting fear from a Closed Internet system is that ISPs (Internet Service Providers) could set up a tiered system thus favoring certain types of traffic and not others or charge fees base on types of use. Imagine web traffic being able to reach one site (because it is large and able to pay) vs. a competing site (which is smaller and unable to pay).

Potential solutions to the issue of Net Neutrality

The idea of Internet service being labeled as a Common Carrierframework. The common carrier framework is also used in the United States for public utilities (electricity, water, etc.), and public transportation. Common carriers are prohibited from interfering with service based on the customer or nature of goods.

Week 3 - Thinking Style

According to the test I have a mind-style of Concrete Random with a close second being Abstract Random. Some of the aspects of the Concrete Random that ring true to me are:

They learn best when...

  • in a personalized environment
  • given broad or general guidelines
  • able to maintain friendly relationships
  • able to participate in group activities

What rings true of the Abstract Random?

  • experimenting to find answers
  • taking risks
  • use their intuition

This is pretty enlightening for me. It gives me a strong insight into how I actually process information and what does and DOESN'T work for me. It's very validating to see what doesn't work for me as well.

The hardest part for me so far has been the reading campaigns - specifically the Elouquent Java page(s). With out a specific reference for the material the reading just passes me by. Like two boats passing at night. I have discovered that if I read for 15 minutes and then do something else I can eventually get through the material. Usually, I really enjoy reading. I just don't initially digest complicated material that way. For me if I can get my hands on something and play with it for a while or have someone walk me through it, I then like to return to a manual or reference material and "fill-in-the-blanks".
